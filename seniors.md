# GrandPad Voice & Tone — Seniors (on-device copy)

## Audience and purpose

This guide governs copy that appears **on the GrandPad device itself** — the screens, buttons, labels, notifications, and messages that older adults read while using their tablet. The reader is the GrandPad owner: an older adult who may be new to tablets, may have some vision or dexterity limitations, and is absolutely capable of using technology when it's designed well for them.

This guide does **not** apply to marketing copy, family-facing emails, or admin dashboards. Those have separate guides.

## Brand foundations

GrandPad's mission: improve the lives of millions of seniors by reconnecting them with their families, friends, and caregivers.

**Brand character.** Everything GrandPad communicates should be:

- **Kind** (friendly, caring, warm, accepting) — not pitying
- **Reassuring** (comforting, encouraging, concerned) — not pandering
- **Present** (attentive, engaged, consistent) — not intrusive
- **Charismatic** (energetic, inspiring, confident) — not boastful
- **Optimistic** (positive, hopeful, vivacious) — not pollyanna
- **Sincere** (frank, open, trustworthy) — not condescending

The "not" half of each pair matters most on-device. Older adults are quick to detect pity, pandering, and condescension — and trust evaporates the moment any of those show up on screen.

**Brand voice.** Four pillars guide how the character above shows up in communication:

- **Kind Reassurance.** Warmth that helps without coddling.
- **Present & Sincere.** Always here, always human. "Hi" beats "Login successful."
- **Charismatic Optimism.** Belief in connection — grounded, never bombastic. On-device, this pillar should be muted; reserve real energy for moments that earn it.
- **Quiet Confidence.** Authority through clarity, not hype.

**Why we communicate.** Every piece of copy should serve at least one of these goals: bring people together, focus on the positive, simplify the complex, or tell people what they get.

## Voice

- **Direct and warm.** Use "you." Greet plainly. Sound like a kind human who respects them.
- **Plain language, always.** If a third-grader couldn't understand it, rewrite. This is the most important rule.
- **Concrete actions.** "Tap the green button to answer." Not "Activate the call connection."
- **Never condescending.** No baby talk. No "easy enough for anyone." No congratulating them for accomplishing basic tasks. They're adults who happen to be older.
- **Short.** Short sentences. Short labels. Short messages. Long copy gets skipped.
- **Reassuring without being sappy.** "Your photo is saved." Not "Wonderful! Your beautiful photo has been saved successfully!"

## How to refer to people

The reader is talking *to* their own family on the device. Use:

- **your family**
- **your loved ones**
- **the person's name** when known: "Call Sarah"
- **your contacts** (functional, when listing them)

Avoid:

- "your seniors" (they ARE the senior)
- "your members"
- "user," "users"
- "family members" (a bit clinical compared to "your family")

## How to refer to the device

The reader knows they're holding their GrandPad. Don't over-explain.

- **GrandPad** when naming it: "Welcome to GrandPad."
- **your tablet** is fine in instructions: "Place your tablet on the charger."
- Don't say "the device" on screen — it sounds robotic.

## Brand

- **GrandPad** — always one word, capital G and P. No ® needed on-device.

## Common words and how to write them

Lean toward the simpler, more familiar version of any pair.

| Use | Not | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **tap** | "click," "press," "select" | Universal touchscreen verb |
| **call** (general action) | "dial," "connect" | Familiar, friendly |
| **video call** (specific noun or action) | "video chat," "video conference" | Use when distinguishing video from voice |
| **photo**, **picture** | "image" | Warmer, more familiar |
| **family** | "contacts" (when referring to people) | Warmer; use "contacts" only as a list label |
| **sign in** | "authenticate," "log in" | Familiar |
| **app** | "application" | Less technical |
| **email** | "e-mail" | Standard form |
| **Wi-Fi** | "wifi" | Hyphenated, capitalized |

## Words to avoid entirely

| Avoid | Why |
| --- | --- |
| simply, just, easily | If it isn't easy for the reader, these words feel like an insult |
| oops! | Patronizing on an error message |
| congrats, awesome, fantastic | Over-celebratory; feels fake |
| "Let's get started!" | Over-energetic |
| any tech jargon | URL, server, sync, authenticate, refresh, cache, etc. If you must explain a concept, do it in plain terms |
| ALL CAPS for emphasis | Reads as shouting and is harder on aging eyes |
| abbreviations | FAQ, etc., e.g., i.e. — spell out or rephrase |

## Sentence patterns that work

Use these as a model when writing new copy:

- **Action prompts:** "Tap [thing] to [outcome]." → "Tap the green button to answer."
- **Confirmations:** "[Thing] saved." / "[Thing] sent." → "Photo saved." "Message sent."
- **Errors:** "[What happened]. [What to do]." → "Wi-Fi disconnected. Tap here to reconnect."
- **Greetings:** "Good morning, [Name]." (warm, not fawning)
- **Empty states:** "[What goes here] will appear here." → "Your photos will appear here."

## Numbers, dates, and time

- Use numerals for clarity at any value: "3 new messages," not "three new messages." (Older eyes scan numerals faster.)
- Time: 12-hour format with AM/PM. "3:00 PM." Avoid 24-hour time.
- Dates: month spelled out. "March 5" or "Tuesday, March 5."
- Avoid relative time like "2h ago" — say "2 hours ago" or "this morning."
- Phone: "555-123-4567."

## Punctuation and capitalization

- **Sentence case** for buttons and headers. "Add a photo," not "Add A Photo."
- **Contractions:** yes. "You're signed in." Warmer than "You are signed in."
- **Exclamation points:** very sparingly. Maximum one per screen, and only when it's truly warranted (a milestone, a celebration).
- **Question marks:** for actual questions. "Are you sure?" works; rhetorical questions don't belong here.
- **Em dash:** avoid on-device. Use commas or two sentences instead — em dashes can be hard to parse at a glance.
- **Quotation marks:** avoid when possible. Use bold or just clear phrasing.

### Periods in UI copy

The rule: **full sentences get periods; fragments don't.** When in doubt, ask whether what's on screen would stand alone as a complete sentence. If yes, period. If it's a label, button, or short status, no period.

| Element | Period? | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Buttons | No | Add a photo |
| Headers and titles | No | Welcome back |
| Form labels | No | Email address |
| Navigation items | No | Photos |
| Single-word or short status | No | Saved &nbsp;·&nbsp; 3 new messages |
| Full-sentence instructions | Yes | Tap the green button to answer. |
| Error messages (full sentence) | Yes | No internet. Tap here to try again. |
| Help text or tooltips (full sentence) | Yes | Your family will see this right away. |
| Body copy and paragraphs | Yes | Standard sentence punctuation throughout. |

Mixing styles within a single screen (some buttons with periods, others without) is the most common mistake. Be consistent.

## Feature names

Feature names on the GrandPad should be capitalized when they're the actual button or section name. Use lowercase when describing the action.

- "Tap Photos to see your album." (Photos is the named section)
- "Tap Call to talk with your family." (Call is the named app)
- "You can share photos with your family." (the action — lowercase)
- "Start a video call to see Sarah's face." (the action — lowercase)

## What "good" looks like

Good:
- "Tap the green button to answer."
- "Photo saved."
- "Your family will see this right away."
- "No internet. Tap here to try again."
- "Welcome back, Eleanor."

Less good (and why):
- "Activate to receive incoming communication." (jargon, robotic)
- "Photo saved successfully!" (unnecessary word + exclamation)
- "Your image has been transmitted to your contacts." (overcomplicated)
- "Oops! Looks like we lost connection." (patronizing)
- "WELCOME BACK!" (shouting)
