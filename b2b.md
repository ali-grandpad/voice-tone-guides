# GrandPad Voice & Tone — B2B

## Audience and purpose

This guide governs **UI and UX copy** in tools used by existing B2B customers and their staff: admin dashboards, configuration screens, partner portals, member-management tools, and operational interfaces. The reader is a healthcare organization administrator, facility staff member, or operations user who works with GrandPad as part of their job.

It does not apply to marketing copy (the website, sales materials, partner-facing collateral), which has its own separate concerns.

## Brand foundations

GrandPad's mission: improve the lives of millions of seniors by reconnecting them with their families, friends, and caregivers. B2B tools support that mission by enabling healthcare partners to deploy, manage, and operate GrandPad effectively for their members.

**Brand character.** Everything GrandPad communicates should be:

- **Kind** (friendly, caring, warm, accepting) — not pitying
- **Reassuring** (comforting, encouraging, concerned) — not pandering
- **Present** (attentive, engaged, consistent) — not intrusive
- **Charismatic** (energetic, inspiring, confident) — not boastful
- **Optimistic** (positive, hopeful, vivacious) — not pollyanna
- **Sincere** (frank, open, trustworthy) — not condescending

When admin UI describes members, "not pitying" and "not condescending" are the most load-bearing pairs — copy that frames members as objects of pity reads poorly to the clinicians and staff who care for them.

**Brand voice.** Four pillars guide how the character above shows up. In admin tools, the emphasis shifts:

- **Quiet Confidence.** Most relevant for B2B UI. Authority through clarity — state what the tool does, what happened, what's required. No hype, no apology.
- **Present & Sincere.** Sound human even in admin copy. "We couldn't reach the device" beats "Device communication exception encountered."
- **Kind Reassurance.** Apply when admin copy touches member-related situations (outages, account locks, billing issues affecting members).
- **Charismatic Optimism.** Muted in B2B UI. Reserve for partner-facing announcements and launches, not day-to-day admin screens.

**Why we communicate.** Every piece of copy should serve at least one of these goals: bring people together, focus on the positive, simplify the complex, or tell people what they get.

## Voice

- **Professional and precise.** Contractions are welcome, but the register leans more formal than consumer copy. This is a workflow tool, not a friendly chat.
- **Healthcare terminology is welcome.** Where clinical, regulatory, or operational terms add precision (admission, discharge, deployment, enrollment, HIPAA, BAA), use them. Don't dumb down for a clinical audience that knows the domain.
- **Direct and concrete.** Tell users what to do, what happened, and what to expect. Avoid filler and vague phrasing.
- **Respectful of expertise.** Don't over-explain steps to someone who runs a facility. Assume competence.
- **Specific over vague.** "Enrollment failed: missing member ID" beats "Something went wrong." Even error messages should be useful.

## How to refer to people

| Use | When |
| --- | --- |
| **member** | The older adult using GrandPad as part of a facility or healthcare program |
| **caregiver**, **staff member**, **care team** | Facility staff and clinicians |
| **admin**, **administrator** | Dashboard users |
| **older adult** | Safe generic default when the population is mixed |

Avoid: "user" (too generic), "patient" (unless clinically accurate), "client," "the elderly," "senior citizen," "worker."

**Note:** Not all GrandPad members are seniors. When the population includes non-senior users (adults with disabilities, recovery patients), default to "member" rather than age-specific terms.

## How to refer to the device

- **GrandPad** (no article) for the brand/product: "GrandPad supports facility-wide deployment."
- **the GrandPad** (with article) for the physical object: "Each member receives a GrandPad upon enrollment."
- **tablet** (lowercase) when device-type clarity matters.
- **device** for technical or troubleshooting contexts: "Restart the device."
- Don't say "GrandPad Tablet" unless it's a formal SKU.

## Brand

- **GrandPad** — always one word, capital G and P. Never "Grandpad," "Grand Pad," or "GRANDPAD." Include ® on first use in marketing materials.

## Words to avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use instead |
| --- | --- | --- |
| elderly / the elderly | Clinical, othering | older adults, seniors, members |
| tech-savvy | Implies a binary; condescending | "comfortable with technology" |
| revolutionary, game-changing, best-in-class | Hype language | State the actual capability |
| we strive to / we're committed to | Vague | State what you actually do |
| rest assured | Corporate filler | Cut, or replace with a specific reassurance |
| simply, just, easily | Often makes readers feel dumb when something isn't | Cut the word |

## Numbers, dates, and time

- Spell out numbers under 10; use numerals for 10 and above.
- Technical/data contexts always use numerals: "5 GB," "3 attempts."
- Time: **[CONFIRM team preference]** — a.m./p.m. with periods, or AM/PM without.
- Dates (admin/dense UI): "2025-03-05" or "Mar 5."
- Phone: "555-123-4567."
- Time zones: PT, ET, CT, MT — all caps, no periods. Use generic (PT) not seasonal (PDT/PST) unless precision is required.
- Multiple time zones: list the reader's likely zone first, others in parentheses. "Webinar starts at 2 PM ET (11 AM PT)."

## Punctuation and capitalization

- **Oxford comma:** always. "Members, families, and caregivers."
- **Headers:** sentence case. "Set up your account," not "Set Up Your Account."
- **Buttons / CTAs:** sentence case, verb-first, under 4 words. "Add a contact," not "Add A New Contact."
- **Contractions:** recommended for warmth.
- **Exclamation points:** sparingly. Maximum one per screen. Never in operational copy.
- **Em dash:** no spaces around it. **[CONFIRM — spaces also acceptable]**
- **Ampersand (&):** avoid in body copy; acceptable in headers, navigation, or proper names.

### Periods in UI copy

The rule: **full sentences get periods; fragments don't.** When in doubt, ask whether the text would stand alone as a complete sentence. If yes, period. If it's a button, label, or short status, no period.

| Element | Period? | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Buttons | No | Enroll member |
| Headers and titles | No | Member details |
| Form labels | No | Member ID |
| Navigation items | No | Reports |
| Single-word or short status | No | Active &nbsp;·&nbsp; 4 pending |
| Full-sentence instructions | Yes | Enter the member's ID to begin enrollment. |
| Error messages (full sentence) | Yes | Enrollment failed: member ID is required. |
| Help text or tooltips (full sentence) | Yes | This ID is used for billing and reporting. |
| Body copy and paragraphs | Yes | Standard sentence punctuation throughout. |

Mixing styles within a single screen (some buttons with periods, others without) is the most common mistake. Be consistent across a workflow.

## Feature names

Capitalize a feature name only when referring to it as a named feature. Use lowercase when describing the action.

- "Open Call to start a video call with the member's family." (named feature)
- "Members can video call up to four contacts at once." (action)
- "Configure Photos access in the member's profile." (named feature)

**Note:** "Video Chat" is not a feature name — both video and voice calls live inside the **Call** app. "Video call" is the right term for the action or noun.
