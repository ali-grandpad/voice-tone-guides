# GrandPad Voice & Tone — B2C

## Audience and purpose

This guide governs **UI and UX copy** in consumer-facing tools used by existing GrandPad customers — primarily the Family Companion app and other consumer-side tools that family members use to manage their loved one's GrandPad, send photos, schedule calls, and so on. It does not apply to marketing copy (the website, ads, sales emails), which has its own separate concerns.

The reader is a family member — often an adult child or grandchild — who is **not assumed to be tech-savvy**. They might be doing this on their lunch break, on a phone they barely know how to use, while feeling some pressure to "get it right" for their loved one. The copy needs to be simple, clear, and warm without being slow or condescending.

## Brand foundations

GrandPad's mission: improve the lives of millions of seniors by reconnecting them with their families, friends, and caregivers. The family-facing UI exists to serve that mission — these are the people choosing GrandPad for their loved ones and managing photos, calls, and content on their behalf.

**Brand character.** Everything GrandPad communicates should be:

- **Kind** (friendly, caring, warm, accepting) — not pitying
- **Reassuring** (comforting, encouraging, concerned) — not pandering
- **Present** (attentive, engaged, consistent) — not intrusive
- **Charismatic** (energetic, inspiring, confident) — not boastful
- **Optimistic** (positive, hopeful, vivacious) — not pollyanna
- **Sincere** (frank, open, trustworthy) — not condescending

The "not pitying" pair is especially important here: family members shouldn't feel that using GrandPad signals decline in their loved one. Avoid copy that frames the senior as a burden, an exception, or someone who "can't handle real technology."

**Brand voice.** Four pillars guide how the character above shows up in communication:

- **Kind Reassurance.** Warmth that helps without coddling. Family members are often worried about their loved ones; reassurance lowers anxiety without sounding saccharine.
- **Present & Sincere.** Sound human, not robotic. "Photo sent to Mom" beats "Upload completed successfully."
- **Charismatic Optimism.** Belief in connection. In family-facing UI, this lives mostly in moments of completion (a sent photo, a scheduled call) — keep it grounded.
- **Quiet Confidence.** Authority through clarity. State what the app does and let it do it.

**Why we communicate.** Every piece of copy should serve at least one of these goals: bring people together, focus on the positive, simplify the complex, or tell people what they get.

## Voice

- **Plain language, always.** Avoid jargon. Don't say "synchronize" when "update" works. Don't say "compatible" when "works with" works.
- **Clear instructions over clever phrasing.** UI copy is read fast, often anxiously. Be obvious about what to do.
- **Warm but not sentimental.** This is a tool, not a Hallmark card. "Photo sent to Mom" is better than "Your beautiful memory is on its way to your loved one!"
- **Not condescending.** Plain doesn't mean dumbed-down. Don't congratulate users for completing basic tasks; don't over-explain.
- **Brevity matters.** Cut every word that doesn't carry weight. If a button label can be three words instead of five, make it three.

## How to refer to people

| Use | When |
| --- | --- |
| **loved one** | The older adult the family is buying GrandPad for |
| **family member**, **mom**, **dad**, **grandma**, **grandpa** | When the relationship is contextually clear |
| **older adult** | Generic, safe default when nothing more specific fits |

Avoid: "user," "your senior," "the elderly," "senior citizen," "elderly relative."

**Special note:** Not all GrandPad recipients are seniors. When you don't know the relationship or the recipient may be a non-senior (adult with a disability, recovery patient), default to "loved one."

## How to refer to the device

- **GrandPad** (no article) for the brand/product: "GrandPad helps families stay connected."
- **the GrandPad** (with article) for the physical object: "Place the GrandPad on the charging cradle."
- **tablet** (lowercase) when device-type clarity matters and "GrandPad" would feel repetitive.
- Don't say "GrandPad Tablet."

## Brand

- **GrandPad** — always one word, capital G and P. Never "Grandpad," "Grand Pad," or "GRANDPAD." Include ® on first use in marketing materials.

## Words to avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use instead |
| --- | --- | --- |
| user | Cold and impersonal in consumer copy | loved one, family member, mom/dad/grandma |
| elderly / the elderly | Clinical, slightly othering | older adult, your loved one |
| tech-savvy / not tech-savvy | Binary, condescending | "comfortable with technology" / "new to technology" |
| easy enough for grandma | Patronizing | "designed to be simple" |
| revolutionary, game-changing, best-in-class | Hype language | State what's actually true |
| simply, just, easily | Often makes readers feel dumb when something isn't | Cut the word; if it's genuinely simple, the brevity will show |
| rest assured | Corporate filler | Cut, or replace with a specific reassurance |
| we strive to / we're committed to | Vague | State what you actually do |

## Numbers, dates, and time

- Spell out numbers under 10; use numerals for 10 and above.
- Technical/data contexts use numerals: "5 GB," "3 minutes."
- Time: **[CONFIRM team preference]** — a.m./p.m. with periods, or AM/PM without.
- Dates: month spelled out. "March 5, 2025."
- Phone: "555-123-4567."
- Time zones: PT, ET, CT, MT — all caps, no periods. Use generic forms (PT) rather than seasonal (PDT/PST) unless precision is required.

## Punctuation and capitalization

- **Oxford comma:** always. "Photos, video calls, and games."
- **Headers:** sentence case. "Set up your account," not "Set Up Your Account."
- **Buttons / CTAs:** sentence case, verb-first, under 4 words. "Add a contact," not "Add A New Contact."
- **Contractions:** non-negotiable. "You're" and "we'll" are warmer than "you are" and "we will."
- **Exclamation points:** sparingly. Maximum one per screen or message. Never to manufacture excitement.
- **Em dash:** no spaces around it. **[CONFIRM — spaces also acceptable]**
- **Ampersand (&):** avoid in body copy; OK in headers or proper names.

### Periods in UI copy

The rule: **full sentences get periods; fragments don't.** When in doubt, ask whether the text would stand alone as a complete sentence. If yes, period. If it's a button, label, or short status, no period.

| Element | Period? | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Buttons | No | Add a contact |
| Headers and titles | No | Set up your account |
| Form labels | No | Email address |
| Navigation items | No | Photos |
| Single-word or short status | No | Sent &nbsp;·&nbsp; 3 new messages |
| Full-sentence instructions | Yes | Tap the green icon to start the call. |
| Error messages (full sentence) | Yes | We couldn't send your photo. Check your connection and try again. |
| Help text or tooltips (full sentence) | Yes | This is the email tied to your account. |
| Body copy and paragraphs | Yes | Standard sentence punctuation throughout. |

Mixing styles within a single screen (some buttons with periods, others without) is the most common mistake. Be consistent across the whole flow.

## Feature names

Capitalize a feature name only when referring to it as a named feature. Use lowercase when describing the action.

- "Open Call to start a video call with your loved one." (named feature)
- "You can video call up to four people at once." (action)
- "Tap Photos to see what's been shared." (named feature)

**Note:** "Video Chat" is not a feature name — both video and voice calls live inside the **Call** app. "Video call" is the right term for the action or noun.
